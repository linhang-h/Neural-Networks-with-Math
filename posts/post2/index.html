What are diffusion models

Diffusion models are a family of generative models. Broadly, these are the models that learn the data distribution by injecting noise via a diffusion process and then generate new samples by denoising.

Diffusion models currently achieve the state-of-the-art performance in image generation and have also seen applications in other tasks.

Score matching

Since g(t)g(t)g(t), μ\muμ and σ\sigmaσ are known, we only need an estimate of ∇xlog⁡pt\nabla_x\log p_t∇x​logpt​ to approximate the time-reserval process. The gradient of log density ∇log⁡p:Rd→Rd\nabla \log p:\mathbb R^d\to \mathbb R^d∇logp:Rd→Rd is known as the score. The idea of learning the score of a data distribution rather than the distribution itself is usually referred to as Score matching.

One major advantage of this idea is that we do not need to normalize our estimate: If we use a neural network f(θ;x)f(\theta;x)f(θ;x) to approximate some density p(x)p(x)p(x), we need to make sure that ∫f(θ;x)dx=1\int f(\theta;x)dx=1∫f(θ;x)dx=1. Computing such an integral can be challenging or even intractable.

For diffusion models, our training objective will be given by

∑i=1nλiE[∥f(θ;xi,ti)−∇xlog⁡pti(xi)∥2], \sum_{i=1}^n\lambda_i\mathbb{E}\left[\left\Vert f(\theta;x_i,t_i) - \nabla_x\log p_{t_i}(x_i)\right\Vert^2\right], i=1∑n​λi​E[∥f(θ;xi​,ti​)−∇x​logpti​​(xi​)∥2],

where (λi)(\lambda_i)(λi​) are positive weights summed up to 111 and determined by the choices of σ\sigmaσ and g(t)g(t)g(t).

Conditioning

In many practical applications of generative models, instead of simply sampling from the data distribution ppp, we want to generate data given certain properties. For example, in image generation, we want an image that fits a certain text discription.

Mathematically, this means that we want to learn the conditional distribution p(⋅∣L)p(\cdot|L)p(⋅∣L) where ℓ\ellℓ is an additional input. In this case, we can still use our noise injection and denoising but the score ∇xlog⁡pt(x)\nabla_x \log p_t(x)∇x​logpt​(x) needs to be replaced with conditional score ∇xlog⁡pt(x∣ℓ)\nabla_x \log p_t(x|\ell)∇x​logpt​(x∣ℓ).

Bayes' rule implies that

∇xlog⁡pt(x∣ℓ)=∇xlog⁡pt(x)+∇xlog⁡pt(ℓ∣x). \nabla_x\log p_t(x|\ell) = \nabla_x\log p_t(x) + \nabla_x \log p_t(\ell|x). ∇x​logpt​(x∣ℓ)=∇x​logpt​(x)+∇x​logpt​(ℓ∣x).

Hence, suppose we have a good estimate for the score. It is suffice to learn the information of ∇xlog⁡pt(ℓ∣x)\nabla_x \log p_t(\ell|x)∇x​logpt​(ℓ∣x). We refer to this method of learning condition score gradient guidance.
